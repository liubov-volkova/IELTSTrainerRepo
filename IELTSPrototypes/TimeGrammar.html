IELTS. How to speak about the time in England?

The 12-hour-system in English

Why we need am/pm?

There is no 24-hour-system in English. That is why in English are used am (Latin: ante meridiem) and pm (post meridiem) in situations where it is necesary to point out that you want to say in the morning or in the evening.

When we can left out am/pm?

When the time of the event is clear, you can left out am/pm (it is choise of the speaker/writer). Usually the context makes the am/pm usage redundant. For example, there is no question that the sunrise is in the morning (e.g. at 04:14) and the sunset is in the evening (e.g. at 10:45).

Time on TV and in timetables

English people don't use the 24-hour clock in every day speech or writing, but they may need to read it when we look at a bus, train or plane timetable. On TV and on the radio this 24-hour time system is used widly and people may need to understand the time watching TV-programs and listening to radio stations. On TV, in timetables they must use the 24-hour-system for writing or saying the time.

How spell the time?

Full hours (o'clock, midnight, midday, noon / ..:00)

"O'clock" is used at full hours. It means no minutes

It can be difficult to say whether 12 o'clock is during the day or the night, that is why English people use two special terms spellin and saying it:

  • 00:00, 24:00 - twelve midnight (or midnight)
  • 12:00 - Twelve noon (or noon, or midday)

When use past, to and say two numbers?

There are two ways of telling the time in English:

  • saying two numbers together (10:40 = It’s ten forty);
  • using past/to (10:40 = It’s twenty to eleven; 11:20 = It's twenty past eleven).

The easiest method is to say two numbers.

The word past is used when the time is between 01 and 30, and to when the time is between 31 and 59.

If there are minutes between ..:.6 and ..:.9, we can use about/around/nearly saying and spelling:

  • It’s about/around/nearly 10, 20 or 30 past .. when the time is between 01 and 30 (e.g. 13:28 = It's nearly thirty minutes past one pm)
  • It’s about/around/nearly to .. when the time is between 31 and 59 (e.g. 13:46 = It's nearly ten minutes to two pm)

On TV and in the timetables we must use the first way using two numbers when saying the time in conjunction with 24-hour-system:

  • 15:46 = It's fifteen fourty six
  • 09:16 = It's nine sixteen

Time and prepositions

There are some rules of using prepositions when we speak about the time:

  1. Preposition at
    • at + specific times (at 4 o’clock, at noon, at twenty five, at two past eleven pm, etc.)
    • at time periods (at the weekend, at the wedding, at midnight, at sunrise, at dinnertime, etc.)
  2. Preposition on
    • on + day (on Monday, on Tuesday, on Wednesdy etc.)
    • on + date (on Christmas day, on New Years, on the 10th October 1977, on the fourth of July, on my birthday, etc.)
  3. Preposition in
    • in + month (in June; in December etc.)
    • in + season (in winter, in summer etc.)
    • in + period of the day (in the evening, in the morning, in the afternoon etc.)
    • in + period (in a few days, in a second etc.)
    • in + year (in the last year; in 2016 etc.)
    • in + long period (in the future, in the past, in the next century etc.)

Time: vocabulary

There are some rules of using prepositions when we speak about the time:

  1. second, minute, hour, day. There are:
    • 60 seconds in a minute
    • 60 minutes in an hour
    • 24 hours in a day
  2. week, work days, weekend, fortnight. There are:
    • 7 days in a week
    • 5 days (Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday, Fiday in England) a work days
    • 2 days (Saturday and Sunday in England) in a weekend
    • 2 weeks in a fortnight
  3. month, quarter, year, leap year, decade, century, milleneum. There are:
    • 28, 29, 30, or 31 days in a month;
    • 3 months in a quarter;
    • 12 months or 365 days in a year;
    • 366 days in a leap year;
    • 10 years in a decade;
    • 100 years in a century;
    • 1,000 years in a millennium;
  4. week There are:
    • 7 days in a week
    • 5 days (Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday, Fiday in England) a work days
    • 2 days (Saturday and Sunday in England) in a weekend
    • 2 weeks in a fortnight
